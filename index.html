Changing Acronyms: CSM → FDE

Customer Success is quietly becoming one of the most technically demanding roles in SaaS. AI didn't create that shift — but it's accelerating it fast enough that the old job title no longer fits.

AUTHOR Bobby Cole
TOPIC Customer Success · AI · SaaS
READ TIME 7 min

There's a piece by Anjor Kanekar that's been rattling around in my head since I read it. He uses a LEGO thought experiment to explain what makes a Forward Deployed Engineer different from a solutions engineer or a consultant. The FDE, he argues, doesn't just build the dragon the customer asked for — they drill holes in bricks, glue things together, hack the inventory, and then walk back to the product team holding a bag of weird custom pieces. The deliverable to the customer is the dragon. The deliverable to your own company is the bag of bricks you had to invent to build it.

I kept reading and thinking: this is just a good CSM.

Not every CSM. Not the ones running QBRs off a slide template and calling it success. But the ones who are actually good at the job — who live in the customer's workflow, figure out what the product can't do yet, stitch together a solution anyway, and bring the signal back to product. That's the job. It's always been the job. We just never called it engineering.

The title is changing. The function was already there. AI is just making the gap between what a great CSM does and what the role is officially supposed to be impossible to ignore.

What CSMs Actually Do (When They're Good)

The standard job description for a Customer Success Manager reads something like: own the relationship, drive adoption, renew the contract, expand where possible. Health scores. QBRs. Playbooks. It's a retention function with a relationship wrapper.

But spend enough time in the role — especially at a technical B2B SaaS company — and the actual job looks very different. You're debugging a client's data integration at 9pm because their board deck is due tomorrow. You're writing SQL to pull a custom report because the product's built-in analytics doesn't slice exactly the way the CFO needs it. You're building a Notion doc that maps your platform's metric definitions to the client's internal glossary because nobody else has done it and it's the only thing standing between a confused portco and a completed IR.

You are, in the Kanekar sense, drilling holes in bricks.

The difference is that in the FDE model, the engineer is explicitly empowered to do that — and explicitly expected to bring the weird bricks back to product. In the CSM model, the drilling happens anyway, but it's invisible. It doesn't show up in the KPIs. It doesn't feed the roadmap in any structured way. And when the CSM leaves, the institutional knowledge walks out with them.

The AI Unlock

Here's where it gets interesting. For most of the history of SaaS customer success, there was a hard ceiling on how technically capable a CSM could be in practice. You could be curious, you could learn SQL, you could hack together a workaround in a spreadsheet — but actually building something for a client required engineering time you didn't have and couldn't justify for a single account.

AI has quietly demolished that ceiling.

1 afternoon time to build a credible bespoke solution that used to require a sprint
0 tickets engineering requests needed when a CSM can write and deploy their own tooling
∞ bricks the library keeps expanding

A CSM who knows how to work with LLMs, APIs, and lightweight automation tools can now do in an afternoon what would have required a full engineering sprint eighteen months ago. They can build a client-specific data parser. They can wire up a Slack alert that pings when a health score dips. They can generate a first draft of a custom benchmark report, personalized to the client's stage and sector, in minutes. They can deploy an AI agent that answers the client's common data questions without routing through a human every time.

Kanekar flags this briefly at the end of his piece — that AI keeps handing you new bricks, and that a single engineer can now build a credible dragon in an afternoon. He notes it blurs the line between FDE and software engineer. What he doesn't fully explore is that it also blurs the line between FDE and CSM — and that for post-sales teams, this is probably the more consequential shift.

The Misaligned Incentives Problem (Sound Familiar?)

Kanekar makes a sharp point about FDE incentives: the moment you start measuring revenue per engagement, your FDEs stop being product scouts and start being account managers. They optimize for charging more for the dragon. They stop bringing back the weird bricks.

CSMs have been living this problem for years — except the incentive isn't per-engagement revenue, it's renewal rate and NPS. Both create the same distortion: optimize for the customer in front of you, at the expense of the signal that would improve the product for everyone.

A CSM who spends three hours building a custom workaround for one client — and doesn't document it, doesn't flag it to product, doesn't ask whether it should exist in the platform — has delivered value to that account and exactly zero value to the company. The dragon was built. The weird bricks were thrown away.

The AI-enabled CSM makes this worse before it makes it better. When building bespoke solutions becomes cheap and fast, the temptation is to just keep building them — to become indispensable through accumulation of custom tooling rather than by pushing the product to absorb the pattern. That's a trap. It's also the fastest way to create a retention risk when that CSM eventually leaves.

The FDE model has a structural answer to this: the bag of bricks feedback loop is a job requirement, not a nice-to-have. Building without bringing back the signal is an incomplete deliverable. Post-sales teams that want to evolve toward this model need to make the same structural commitment — which means KPIs that reward product insight, not just retention metrics, and a real channel from CSMs to product that isn't just a Slack message into the void.

What the Role Actually Becomes

If you follow this logic out, the AI-enabled CSM starts to look less like a relationship manager and more like an embedded product engineer who happens to be deployed on the customer side. They're technical enough to build real things. They're close enough to the customer to know what's actually broken. And they're in a structurally unique position — sitting at the intersection of customer need and product capability — to see patterns that neither pure engineers nor pure salespeople will ever see.

This isn't a new archetype in enterprise software — Palantir built an entire company around the FDE model, and the results speak for themselves. What's new is that the tooling now exists to make this model accessible outside of Palantir-scale engineering organizations. A Series B SaaS company with a five-person CS team can now have one or two people operating in this way — building real things, closing real gaps, feeding real signal back to product — without hiring a dedicated forward deployment engineering org.

The question isn't whether this model is better. It clearly is, for the right kind of company. The question is whether the CSMs who are already operating this way — quietly drilling bricks, stitching together solutions, bringing back signal that never gets formally captured — will be recognized for what they're actually doing. And whether the companies they work for will build the structures that let that work compound, instead of evaporating every time someone takes a new job.

So What's in a Name?

Titles matter less than the actual work — but they're not nothing. A "Customer Success Manager" title signals a certain set of expectations: relationship-first, renewals-focused, reactive by default. A "Forward Deployed Engineer" title signals something different: technical, product-aware, proactive about closing gaps. Same underlying capability, different organizational contract.

The companies that figure this out first will have a meaningful structural advantage. Not because FDE is a magic title, but because naming the function correctly forces the org to support it correctly — with the right KPIs, the right product feedback channels, the right hiring profiles, and the right expectation that the person in this seat is doing two jobs simultaneously: delivering for the customer in front of them, and building the intelligence that improves the product for everyone else.

I don't think every CSM should become an FDE. The relationship and trust-building dimension of customer success is genuinely valuable and genuinely distinct — not every client wants their CSM to show up with a laptop and start hacking. Some clients just want to feel heard, and that's a real skill that has nothing to do with engineering.

But the best CSMs I've seen — the ones who drive outsized retention, who become genuinely trusted advisors to client C-suites, who seem to always know what the product needs before the product team does — they're already operating as FDEs. They just don't have the title. Or the bag to put the weird bricks in.

AI didn't create this archetype. It just made it viable at scale — and made the cost of not investing in it high enough to finally notice. The acronym change is a symptom. The underlying shift is that post-sales is becoming an engineering function. The sooner organizations build around that reality, the better.

Fantasy Football Taught Me How to Work With VC Clients

Sports analytics has been running laps around enterprise data for decades. Here's what ten years of fantasy football taught me about portfolio intelligence — and why your VC firm should care.

AUTHOR Bobby Cole
TOPIC Data · VC · Portfolio Strategy
READ TIME 7 min

I've been in three fantasy football leagues for over ten years. That's a decade of draft boards, trade negotiations, waiver wire pickups at midnight, and more spreadsheet time than I'd like to admit. At some point I stopped thinking of it as a hobby and started recognizing it for what it actually was: a live training ground for data-driven decision-making under uncertainty.

Sports analytics has been ahead of the enterprise curve for a long time. Billy Beane was building expected value models while most Fortune 500 companies were still running on gut instinct and quarterly reports. The NBA revolutionized player efficiency ratings before "data-driven culture" became a corporate buzzword. Sports front offices were doing predictive modeling, regression analysis, and portfolio construction long before those words showed up in VC pitch decks.

So when I started working with VC and PE firms — organizations sitting on some of the richest longitudinal company data in existence — I kept noticing the same pattern: the raw material for world-class analytics was all there. What was missing was the framework to use it.

Fantasy football is, at its core, a portfolio management problem. You draft assets, monitor performance, manage risk, make bets on upside, and cut your losses when the data tells you to. Sound familiar?

The Draft Is Your Investment Thesis

Every fantasy season starts with a draft. You have a finite number of picks, a finite budget of roster slots, and a market of available players — each with a price implied by their draft position. The best managers don't just chase last year's stats. They find players whose underlying metrics suggest the box score hasn't caught up yet. Targets per route run. Yards after contact. Red zone opportunity share. The surface number is the outcome. The analytics tell you whether it's repeatable.

VC firms do the same thing at the fund level — but the data infrastructure to support it often lags. A firm building a new fund thesis needs to know which metrics actually predicted outlier outcomes in their prior portfolio, not just which companies had the best-looking revenue curves. Leading indicators over lagging ones. Process over results. The firms I work with that operate this way make better decisions at every stage — from initial check to follow-on to board-level resource allocation.

In-Season Management Is Active Portfolio Work

Drafting well gets you maybe 60% of the way there. The other 40% is in-season management — which is where most people either win or lose their leagues. This is where the data discipline separates the serious managers from the casual ones.

The waiver wire is the most underrated tool in fantasy. Every week, a set of players becomes available — some because of injury, some because of emerging opportunity, some because their previous owner gave up too early. The best managers are running the same math on a Tuesday night that a good VC runs before a bridge round: what's the expected value here? What am I giving up to get it? What does the data say about the upside versus the risk?

3 leagues running simultaneously for 10+ years — each with different scoring systems, roster formats, and competitive dynamics
Leading indicators beat lagging ones every time — in fantasy and in portfolio monitoring
The Wire waiver decisions mirror follow-on investment logic — triage, expected value, opportunity cost

For a VC portfolio, the waiver wire equivalent is the health monitoring layer — the system that flags which companies are showing early signs of stress before it becomes a crisis, and which ones are quietly outperforming before anyone notices. The firms with this infrastructure make better triage decisions. They know where to allocate partner time. They don't get surprised.

The "Eye Test" Problem

Every fantasy manager has that one guy in the league who trusts his gut over the numbers. He watched the game, he has a feel for the player, he just knows. And sometimes he's right — which makes him impossible to reason with for the rest of the season.

The VC equivalent is the partner who has a feel for a founder. Gut instinct is real and valuable — pattern recognition built over decades of sourcing deals is not nothing. But gut and data are most powerful together, not in opposition. The firms I've seen operate at the highest level use data to stress-test conviction, not replace it. They go into a board meeting knowing the numbers already tell a story — and they use that story to ask better questions, not to skip the conversation.

Portfolio Construction Is Not Just About Stars

The most common mistake in fantasy is over-indexing on upside at the expense of floor. You load up on boom-or-bust players chasing the big week, and then you lose by one point in the semifinals because three of them had bye weeks and your bench was an afterthought.

VC portfolio construction has the same tension. The moonshot investments are necessary — that's the asset class. But the firms with the most durable performance think carefully about the shape of the whole portfolio: concentration risk, stage diversification, sector exposure, follow-on reserves. It's not just about picking winners. It's about building a roster that can absorb variance and still compete.

The best fantasy managers I know don't just optimize for the best possible week. They optimize for the best possible season — which means making decisions that protect against downside, not just chase upside. That distinction matters more than most people realize.

What Sports Got Right That Enterprise Is Still Learning

Sports analytics normalized something that enterprise data culture is still wrestling with: the idea that historical outcomes are a lagging indicator, and that the real edge lives in process metrics. Completion percentage over touchdown totals. Routes run over receptions. Yards before contact over yards after.

Exhibit A: Howie Roseman and the Eagles

I'd be lying if I said this wasn't personal. I'm an Eagles fan. And watching Howie Roseman quietly build one of the most analytics-forward front offices in the NFL over the past two to three years has been — professionally, not just emotionally — deeply validating.

While most NFL franchises were still treating analytics as a supplementary function, Roseman was restructuring the Eagles' entire decision-making infrastructure around it. He made a sustained, deliberate commitment to hiring data analysts — not as support staff, but as core contributors to draft evaluation, contract modeling, and roster construction. He brought in people with backgrounds in quantitative finance and applied statistics. He built systems that could surface the kind of signal that doesn't show up in a box score until it's already too late to act on.

The Eagles didn't start winning because they got lucky. They started winning because they built the infrastructure to make better decisions — systematically, repeatedly, at scale. That's not a hot streak. That's a compounding advantage.

The results followed. Consecutive Super Bowl appearances. A roster built on undervalued contracts, late-round draft hits, and trades that looked questionable until the data said otherwise. Roseman didn't just talk about analytics — he institutionalized it. And I'd argue that distinction is exactly what separates the VC firms I most respect from the ones still waiting for the quarterly IR to tell them what the leading indicators already knew.

2–3 yrs of deliberate analytics investment under Roseman before the results became undeniable
Infrastructure wins. Not a single great pick — a system that consistently finds signal others miss
Fly Eagles Fly obligatory. but also: this is what it looks like when a front office takes data seriously

In a VC portfolio context, this translates directly. Revenue is a lagging indicator. So is churn. The leading signals — product engagement depth, net revenue retention trends, sales cycle velocity, hiring patterns — tell you what's coming six to twelve months before it shows up in the headline number. The firms that have built the data infrastructure to surface those signals consistently are playing a different game than the ones waiting for the quarterly IR to arrive.

I've spent years helping VC and PE clients build exactly that infrastructure. Defining KPIs that actually predict outcomes. Creating health frameworks with automated alerts. Building benchmarking systems that let a portfolio company understand where they stand before a fundraise — not during one. The playbook isn't that different from what a good fantasy manager does every week. You're just managing a more consequential roster.

Ten years of fantasy football didn't make me a better analyst in any direct sense. But it did build a reflex — the habit of asking what the data actually says before acting on what feels right, of looking for the metric behind the metric, of treating every decision as a portfolio problem with tradeoffs rather than an isolated judgment call.

Sports figured this out decades ago. The best VC firms are getting there. The ones who close the gap fastest — who build the data infrastructure to see their portfolios the way a front office sees a roster — will have a structural advantage that compounds every year. I'm convinced of that. And I'd bet a waiver claim on it.
